📖 Read the chapter

Probability measures how likely an event is, from 0 (impossible) to 1 (certain). For equally likely outcomes it's favourable ÷ total.

Probability measures how likely an event is, on a scale from 0 (impossible) to 1 (certain).

  • The sample space is the full list of equally likely outcomes for an experiment — get this list right before calculating anything. For example, rolling one die has sample space 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6.
  • For equally likely outcomes: P(event) = favourable outcomes ÷ total outcomes. For example, P(even number on a die) = 3 ÷ 6 = ½.
  • The complement rule: P(not A) = 1 − P(A) — this often turns a hard "at least one" question into an easy one. For example, P(rain) = 0.3, so P(no rain) = 1 − 0.3 = 0.7.
  • For two events together, "AND" multiplies and "OR" adds — but mind the conditions: multiply only when the events are independent (one doesn't affect the other), add only when they cannot both happen at once. Two separate coins both landing heads: ½ × ½ = ¼, matching HH being 1 of the 4 equally likely outcomes.
  • A probability is not tested by one trial. "30% chance of rain" not raining doesn't make the forecast wrong — probabilities are checked over many repeats, comparing the predicted probability to the relative frequency (how often it actually happened). For example, 200 spins landing red 50 times gives a relative frequency of 50 ÷ 200 = ¼.
  • Each toss, roll or spin is independent of the last. If a coin has landed tails five times running, heads is still exactly 50% likely next time — it is never "due".

🎲 Rolling two dice

Wanting a total of 7: the sample space has 6×6 = 36 equally likely outcomes, and 6 of them make 7. So P(total = 7) = 6⁄36 = 1⁄6.

🌤️ Reading a weather forecast

A forecast gives a 30% chance of rain. P(rain) = 0.3, so P(no rain) = 1 − 0.3 = 0.7 — more likely than not, but rain is still a real possibility.

🪙 Tossing two coins

It's tempting to guess 3 outcomes (0, 1 or 2 heads). The true sample space has 4: HH, HT, TH, TT — so "exactly one head" is 2⁄4 = 1⁄2, not 1⁄3.

Why list all outcomes? Probability compares outcomes you want against every outcome possible. For combined events, a sample space keeps you honest.
⚠ Watch outFavourable ÷ total only works when outcomes are equally likely. For “at least one”, complementary counting is usually easier.
⚡ Mental maths strategyFor “at least one…”, go through the back door: find the chance of none, then subtract from 1.

HardChallenge scenario
Two coins are tossed. Find P(exactly one head) and P(at least one head).
  1. Sample space (4 equally likely): HH, HT, TH, TT.
  2. Exactly one head — HT and TH — is 2/4 = 1/2.

🕵️ Mistake detective

Diagnose the misconception, then reveal the cause and intervention.

A fair die shows a 5 with probability 5/6.
Cause

They used the number on the face instead of counting outcomes.

Diagnostic question

What rule or representation would show why this answer cannot be right?

Intervention

There is 1 way to roll a 5 out of 6 faces, so P(5) = 1/6.
