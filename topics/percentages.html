👀 Notice & wonder

Before any rules: just look. What do you notice? What do you wonder?

The same $80 jacket in two shops: one sign says “25% off”, the other says “$20 off”.

You might notice…

  • One is written as a rate, the other as an amount
You might wonder…

  • What is 25% actually of?

Hold on to those wonderings — this chapter answers most of them.

🔮 Discover — predict first

25% means 25 out of 100. What simple fraction is that? What decimal?

25/100 = 1/4 = 0.25. Percent, fraction and decimal are three views of one amount.

📖 Read the chapter

'Per cent' means 'out of 100'. A percentage is a fraction with denominator 100, so 25% = 25/100 = 1/4 — linking straight to fractions and decimals.

"Per cent" means per hundred — a percentage is a fraction locked to a denominator of 100, so it converts freely between forms.

  • 25% = 25⁄100 = 0.25 = 1⁄4 — the same amount, written three different ways.
  • To turn any percentage into a decimal, divide by 100 (move the point two places left): 37% = 0.37, 6.5% = 0.065. To go the other way, multiply by 100 (move it two places right): 0.6 = 60%.
  • To find a percentage of an amount: (percent ÷ 100) × amount. For example, 20% of 150 = (20 ÷ 100) × 150 = 30.
  • A percentage is always taken of a particular amount, called the base. Change the base, and the same percentage becomes a different actual amount. For example, 10% of $50 is $5, but 10% of $80 is $8.
  • Percentage changes do not simply add: a 10% rise followed by a 10% fall does not return you to the start, because the second 10% is of a different amount. For example, $100 rises 10% to $110, then falls 10% to $99 — not back to $100.
  • Working backwards: if you know the price after a discount and need the original, the sale price is not 100% — it is (100 − discount)% of the original. Divide by that percentage (as a decimal) to undo it. For example, a jacket costs $60 after 25% off, so $60 is 75% of the original: $60 ÷ 0.75 = $80.
  • To find the whole when you know a percentage of it, use the 1% method: divide by that percentage to get 1%, then multiply by 100. If 15% of a number is 45, then 1% = 45 ÷ 15 = 3, so 100% = 300.

🏷️ Shop sale

An $80 jacket is 25% off. 25% of 80 = (25÷100)×80 = $20 off, so it now costs $60.

🍽️ Restaurant tip

A $45 bill, 10% tip: 10% of $45 = $4.50. Knowing 10% (just divide by 10) makes any tip or discount easy to estimate in your head.

📈📉 Rise then fall

A $100 item rises 10% to $110, then falls 10%. 10% of $110 = $11, so it drops to $99 — not back to $100, because the second 10% was taken of a bigger number.

🔙 Working out the original price

A jacket is on sale for $60 after 25% off. $60 is not the whole jacket — it's what's left after removing a quarter, so $60 is 75% of the original.

Original = 60 ÷ 0.75 = $80. Taking 25% off $60 (giving $45) would be the wrong direction — that undoes a discount that was never applied to $60 in the first place.

Why 10% off twice ≠ 20% off? The second discount comes off a smaller price. A percentage is always a fraction of a particular amount.
⚠ Watch outPercentages don’t simply add across steps: a 10% rise then a 10% fall lands below the start, because each is taken of a different amount.
⚡ Mental maths strategyBuild everything from 10% (just divide by 10): 35% = three 10%s plus half of one. Bonus: a% of b always equals b% of a, so 8% of 25 = 25% of 8 = 2.

🕵️ Mistake detective

Diagnose the misconception, then reveal the cause and intervention.

An $80 coat with 25% off costs $25.
Cause

They found the discount but forgot to subtract it from the price.

Diagnostic question

What rule or representation would show why this answer cannot be right?

Intervention

25% of 80 = $20 off, so the coat costs 80 − 20 = $60.

🌍 In the real world

Sales, tips, GST, test marks and loan interest are all percentages. Knowing 10% (just divide by 10) lets you estimate any tip or discount in your head.
