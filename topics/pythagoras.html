🔮 Discover — predict first

Build squares on the two short sides of a 3-4-5 triangle (areas 9 and 16). Add them. What's the area of the square on the longest side?

9 + 16 = 25, and 5² = 25. The squares on the two shorter sides always add to the square on the hypotenuse.

📖 Read the chapter

In a right-angled triangle, the square of the hypotenuse equals the sum of the squares of the other two sides: a² + b² = c².

In a right-angled triangle, the side opposite the right angle — the longest — is the hypotenuse. Pythagoras' theorem ties the three side lengths together exactly.

Theorem & conversea² + b² = c²
and if a²+b²=c², the angle IS right

One way to see why: draw squares on all three sides — the two smaller squares' areas rebuild precisely into the big one. The converse also works, so the rule both finds a missing side and tests whether a triangle is right-angled. Sets like 3-4-5 and 5-12-13 are worth memorising.

Why it matters: it links all three sides, so knowing two always finds the third — used in construction, navigation and screen sizes.
⚠ Watch outThe theorem works only for right-angled triangles, and c must be the hypotenuse — the longest side, opposite the right angle.

🕵️ Mistake detective

Diagnose the misconception, then reveal the cause and intervention.

Legs 3 and 4, so the hypotenuse is 3 + 4 = 7.
Cause

They added the sides instead of the squares of the sides.

Diagnostic question

What rule or representation would show why this answer cannot be right?

Intervention

c² = 3² + 4² = 9 + 16 = 25, so c = √25 = 5.

🌍 In the real world

Builders check a corner is square with the 3-4-5 rule; TV sizes, ladder safety and GPS distances all use Pythagoras.
