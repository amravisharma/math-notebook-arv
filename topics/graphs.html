🔮 Discover — predict first

Plot (3, 2) and (−3, 2). They share the same height but sit in different quadrants. What makes them different?

The x-signs differ: (3, 2) is in Quadrant I, (−3, 2) in Quadrant II. The pair of signs (x, y) fixes the quadrant.

📖 Read the chapter

The Cartesian plane locates points with (x, y). A straight-line graph like y = 2x + 1 shows every point that makes the equation true.

The coordinate plane pairs every point with (x, y): across, then up. A straight line is the set of all points that satisfy a linear equation — each point on it makes the equation true, and no others do.

Line equationy = mx + c
m = gradient = rise ÷ run
c = y-intercept

The gradient m is a rate of change — how much y climbs per step of x — and c fixes where the line meets the y-axis. Together they pin the line down completely; parallel lines share the same gradient.

What the numbers mean: in y = mx + c, m is the gradient (steepness = rise ÷ run) and c is where it crosses the y-axis. Those two numbers draw the whole line.
⚠ Watch outGradient is rise ÷ run (Δy ÷ Δx), not run ÷ rise. A vertical line has an undefined gradient, not zero.

🕵️ Mistake detective

Diagnose the misconception, then reveal the cause and intervention.

They plotted (3, 2) by going up 3, then right 2.
Cause

Coordinates are (x, y): the x comes first — across before up.

Diagnostic question

What rule or representation would show why this answer cannot be right?

Intervention

Go right 3 (x), then up 2 (y). Always read across before up.

🌍 In the real world

Maps, GPS, game screens and every chart you've ever seen use coordinates to pin down a position.
